Case Study: AskAgent — Building a RAG-Based LLM Support Tool from Zero to 90% Ticket Deflection
Company Context
Higharc is a Series B–C PropTech platform that automates residential home design and construction documentation for production homebuilders. The platform is highly technical — builders use it to generate architectural plans, configure home models, and manage construction data across complex workflows.
As Head of Customer Experience, I led a 60-person global organization spanning Implementation, Customer Success, Training, Support, and Professional Services. Our customer base consisted of enterprise homebuilders averaging $350K+ ARR, running multi-year implementations with steep learning curves.
The problem was clear: Our support and training teams were drowning in repetitive, process-oriented questions — "How do I configure a roof in Studio?" or "What's the workflow for setting up a new community?" — while our subject matter experts were pulled into firefighting instead of scaling enablement. We needed a way to capture institutional knowledge and make it instantly accessible, without adding headcount linearly.

The Challenge
Higharc's platform was evolving rapidly. New features shipped monthly. Training content couldn't keep pace with product releases, and tribal knowledge lived in the heads of a handful of senior implementation specialists. The downstream effects:

Support ticket volume was climbing as the customer base grew, with a significant percentage of tickets being procedural "how-to" questions
Training resources were bottlenecked — we had limited SMEs and high demand for both live and async enablement
Time-to-value suffered because customers waited on human answers to questions that should have been self-serve
Internal team productivity eroded as experienced staff spent cycles answering the same questions repeatedly

I needed a solution that could absorb our domain expertise, serve it back accurately in real-time, and scale without burning out the team.

The Solution: AskAgent
I designed and led the development of AskAgent, a custom RAG-based (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) LLM chatbot built on Replit, purpose-built to serve as Higharc's AI-powered support and enablement engine.
Phase 1: Knowledge Capture & Architecture
We built the application in Replit as a full-stack web app with a knowledge management backend and a multi-model chat interface. The core architecture:

Knowledge Block System: A structured submission interface where team members could input discrete units of domain knowledge — step-by-step task instructions, workflow explanations, configuration guides, and troubleshooting procedures. Each block was tagged by product area, complexity level, and use case.
Transcription Engine: We integrated a transcription pipeline directly into Replit to convert our library of recorded training sessions (125+ sessions, 500+ learners over the program's life) into searchable, structured text. This was critical — years of accumulated expertise lived in video recordings that were effectively inaccessible for RAG retrieval until we transcribed and chunked them.
PDF Upload & Processing: The system accepted bulk PDF uploads of existing documentation, SOPs, and guides, parsing them into knowledge blocks automatically.
Multi-Model Chat Interface: The chatbot integrated 8 selectable LLM models across Claude, OpenAI, and Gemini families. This wasn't vanity — it was strategic. Model performance varied dramatically depending on the task.

Phase 2: Content Sprint — 1,000 Knowledge Blocks in 90 Days
We set an aggressive target: 1,000 knowledge blocks submitted in three months. This wasn't a side project — it was a department-wide initiative. I embedded it into team OKRs and made knowledge block contribution a trackable KPI on our department dashboard.
The content came from three sources:

Training transcriptions — Our existing library of recorded sessions, transcribed and decomposed into discrete instructional blocks
Manual expert submissions — Implementation specialists, trainers, and support engineers submitted step-by-step procedures and configuration guides one by one
PDF documentation — Existing SOPs, product guides, and process docs uploaded and parsed into the knowledge base

We tracked knowledge block volume as a department-level health metric, with a target of 30%+ quarter-over-quarter growth (starting baseline: 695 blocks at the beginning of Q3 2025, targeting 800).
Phase 3: Validation Hackathon
Once we had our initial knowledge base populated, I organized a structured hackathon with 16 users from across my Implementation and Training organization. The hackathon had a specific, methodical purpose:
The Protocol:

Each participant was assigned a set of realistic customer questions and support scenarios
They prompted AskAgent and evaluated the responses against known-correct answers
Every response was scored: Accurate, Partially Accurate, or Inaccurate/Insufficient
Inaccurate results were flagged with specific notes on what went wrong — missing context, wrong steps, hallucinated information, outdated procedures
Flagged items were routed back to knowledge block owners for immediate refinement

This wasn't just QA — it was a human-in-the-loop training cycle. The hackathon surfaced systemic gaps we couldn't have found through random testing.
Phase 4: Model Selection & Cost Optimization
With 8 models available, we ran continuous evaluation across three dimensions:

Output quality: Accuracy, completeness, and coherence of responses
Processing capability: Ability to handle large context windows (training transcripts, multi-page SOPs)
Cost efficiency: Token costs at scale

Key finding: Gemini's models consistently outperformed on large text document processing and information synthesis — critical for our use case where knowledge blocks often referenced lengthy training transcriptions and multi-step procedures. We used this insight to set intelligent defaults while keeping model switching available for edge cases.
The model landscape shifted constantly. What worked best in month one wasn't necessarily optimal in month three as providers released updates. We built the multi-model architecture specifically to stay agile rather than locked into a single vendor.
Phase 5: The Additive vs. Subtractive Training Breakthrough
The most significant learning — and one that fundamentally shaped our approach — was discovering that training a RAG-based LLM requires both additive and subtractive methods.
The Additive Approach (what most teams default to):

Adding more knowledge blocks to fill gaps
Expanding existing blocks with additional context and edge cases
Uploading new documentation as it was created
Transcribing additional training sessions

The Subtractive Approach (the counterintuitive breakthrough):

Deleting knowledge blocks that contained outdated, contradictory, or overly generic information
Pruning specific steps within blocks that were causing the LLM to hallucinate or generate confidently wrong answers
Removing redundant blocks where overlapping content created retrieval confusion — the model would pull from multiple similar-but-not-identical sources and synthesize an inaccurate hybrid response
Stripping irrelevant context that diluted the signal-to-noise ratio in retrieval

This was the hardest lesson. The instinct is always to add more data. But we found that a curated, precise knowledge base dramatically outperformed a bloated one. When the LLM hallucinated, the fix was often removing the content that was leading it astray, not adding more content to overpower it.
We documented this as an internal framework: "Additive fills gaps. Subtractive fixes hallucinations." The team learned to diagnose whether a bad response was caused by missing information (add) or conflicting/noisy information (subtract).

Key Learnings

Treat knowledge curation like product management. Knowledge blocks aren't static documentation — they're a living product that requires roadmapping, QA, iteration, and deprecation. We tracked them as a department KPI, not a nice-to-have.
Subtractive training is as important as additive. The instinct to "just add more data" can actually degrade LLM performance. Contradictory, redundant, or outdated content causes hallucinations. Pruning is a discipline, not a failure.
Multi-model architecture is a strategic advantage, not a luxury. LLM capabilities and pricing shift constantly. Being model-agnostic meant we could optimize for quality and cost simultaneously without rebuilding infrastructure.
Hackathons are structured QA, not team-building exercises. Our 16-person validation sprint wasn't about morale — it was a systematic human-in-the-loop evaluation that surfaced patterns no automated testing would catch.
Transcription is an underrated knowledge unlock. Years of institutional expertise was trapped in video recordings. Building a transcription pipeline into our knowledge ingestion workflow converted dead assets into high-value RAG content.
Embed AI initiatives into existing OKRs. AskAgent succeeded because knowledge block contribution was a tracked team metric, not a voluntary side project. Adoption follows accountability.


Technical Stack

Platform: Replit (full-stack web application)
LLM Integration: 8 selectable models across OpenAI, Claude (Anthropic), and Google Gemini
Knowledge Architecture: Custom knowledge block system with tagging, versioning, and contributor tracking
Content Ingestion: Transcription engine (audio/video → structured text), PDF parser, manual submission interface
Retrieval: RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) with vector search across knowledge blocks
Monitoring: Dashboard tracking knowledge block volume, usage rates, and response quality metrics


Why This Matters
AskAgent wasn't a science project. It was a direct response to the core scaling challenge every CX organization faces: How do you maintain quality and speed as your customer base grows without hiring linearly?
The answer is to systematize institutional knowledge into an AI-powered layer that handles the 90% of questions that are procedural, while freeing your experts to focus on the 10% that require judgment, relationship, and strategic thinking.
This project demonstrated that a non-engineering CX leader can architect, build, and deploy production-grade AI tooling that delivers measurable business outcomes — 90% ticket deflection, faster time-to-value, and a scalable knowledge infrastructure that compounds over time.
